Exhibition Highlights

01. Shop Grinders
The large grinders you first encounter at the entrance to the third-floor gallery were used in European shops in the early 1900s. At that time, Europe saw coffee consumption surge, much as it has in Korea today, and shops began selling beans directly to customers. Unlike household grinders, these models adopted a side-driven mechanism that distributed force efficiently when grinding large quantities of hard beans. Their bodies were made of smooth cherry wood, and some were topped with trophy-shaped brass ornaments so they could also serve as elegant shop displays. Later versions used cast-metal bodies and flywheels. The olive-colored cast grinder displayed at the back is a field model used by the German military into the 1980s; it weighs a hefty 9 kg and could take 250 g of beans at a time.

02. Toy Grinders for Children
The display case on the left side of the gallery is filled with grinders so small they look like miniatures. These, however, are not decorative souvenirs but toys made for children. Before TV or the internet existed, moving household objects were fascinating playthings. Parents would watch with delight as children put in a few coffee beans, try grinding them, and smell the aroma as part of their pretend play. Surprisingly, because these pieces were made by specialist coffee-grinder manufacturers of the day, they are small but fully functional. One model made in 1939 even has a contemporary coin attached to it, vividly confirming the period in which it was produced.

03. The Birth of Coffee Tools Designed with Women's Tastes in Mind
Wall-mounted ceramic grinders reflect the social atmosphere of early twentieth-century Europe. Coffee had previously been regarded as a drink for men, but as women's rights expanded, women began drinking coffee as well. In response, leading ceramics companies such as Villeroy & Boch introduced grinders with beautiful ceramic storage hoppers designed to suit women's tastes. At a time when airtight bean containers were not common, storing a measured quantity of beans in a ceramic wall container and grinding them as needed was both practical and decorative, so the form became popular across Europe. Another point of interest is comparing the ceramic labels for "coffee" written in the different languages of Germany, France, the Netherlands, and beyond.

04. Various Wall-Mounted Grinders
The black cast-iron grinder is the "Spong" model, produced in Britain from 1895 onward. Not only was it well designed, it was also remarkably advanced for its time, adopting flat burrs of the kind then associated with electric grinders and capable of producing exceptionally fine particles.

(Image source: https://www.home-barista.com/)

The cast grinder in military green with an aluminum hopper was made for the Swiss military. It was specially designed so it could be disassembled and carried easily by Swiss troops, who often moved across borders.

The grinder with the rather large ceramic container has a lever that lets the user choose between grinding barley (Malz) and coffee (Bohnen). A divider inside the ceramic hopper allowed both coffee beans and roasted barley to be stored together. After the First World War, coffee was hard to obtain in Europe, and people roasted barley as a substitute; this object preserves both the hardship and the ingenuity of that moment.

05. A Guinness-Record Exhibit - The Lehnartz Collection
Here, 350 coffee grinders made by the German specialist manufacturer Lehnartz, which produced 100,000 grinders a year in the 1930s, are gathered in one place. The Lehnartz family, blacksmiths for six generations, was one of Europe's largest coffee-grinder makers and produced popular goods throughout the continent under a red rooster logo.

Most notably, about 220 of the pieces shown here were collected over 40 years by the German printer Joeg Fridrich. Among his collection, 210 examples earned him a place in the Guinness Book of Records in 2001 as the person who collected the greatest number of Lehnartz coffee grinders. Fridrich carefully noted the purchase date, place, and price on the base plate of each grinder. After his death in 2005, the collection was transferred to Director Lee Seung-jae, and it is now held by the Mahlwerk exhibition hall.

06. The Company That Made Coffee Grinders Before Cars - Peugeot
As coffee demand surged in the nineteenth century, coffee grinders also entered mass production. The company that built a factory for large-scale coffee-grinder production was France's Peugeot, which later became a world-famous car maker. Peugeot was producing coffee grinders in 1840, fifty years before it began making automobiles in 1890. Unlike earlier handicraft products, Peugeot simplified the structure and adopted a modern production method that made assembly easy. The lion still used today as Peugeot's emblem can already be seen clearly on the company's earliest models.

07. Electric Coffee Grinders Changed the Paradigm
After coffeehouses appeared across Europe in the seventeenth century, dedicated coffee grinders were produced, and manual grinders accompanied European coffee culture for more than 300 years. In the early twentieth century, however, advances in materials and technology brought new designs and mechanisms: plastic bodies, aluminum handles, stainless-steel lids, and more. This technical evolution, together with coffee's growing popularity, seemed to promise a splendid new age of manual coffee grinders. Yet the arrival of electric coffee grinders in the 1950s instead brought that era to a close. In the age of manual grinding, blacksmiths who made burrs played a central role; in the electric age, that role shifted to companies that made motors. This was also when most manual coffee-grinder manufacturers, many of them centuries old, closed their doors almost at once.

08. The "Fragrance of Peace" Cast from the Scars of War
The brass grinder displayed here has the year 1939 clearly engraved on its base, the very year the Second World War broke out. Fearing the horrors of war, a skilled craftsperson began collecting unexploded shells and cartridge cases scattered across battlefields. He melted down the brass from those instruments of death and remade it into a coffee grinder that offered people a fragrant moment of rest. He even cut one cartridge case to make the grounds drawer and used the nose of a shell as decoration so the object's origin would not be forgotten. The maker's earnest cry, "Stop the war and create an age of peace in which we drink coffee," still seems to remain in this brass glow even now, more than eighty years later.

09. Zassenhaus, 150 Years of Design Excellence
Since 1867, the German company Zassenhaus has produced coffee grinders and is widely regarded as a premium name in the field. It had already established mass-production processes and technical leadership long before coffee first entered the Korean royal court. Especially noteworthy is a design the company introduced in 1950 that still looks astonishingly modern and refined today. With a cherry-wood body, a stainless-steel lid, and a sleek aluminum handle, this model was given the proper name "Brilliant" instead of being identified only by a model number. We invite you to enjoy the Zassenhaus design language, where utility rises into artistry.

10. The Birth of the First Plastic Coffee Grinder
In 1931, the German firm Peter Dienes introduced what is considered the world's first plastic coffee grinder. It used Bakelite, one of the earliest synthetic polymer materials, which had only recently been developed. Bakelite first appeared in 1905, but it was not used for everyday consumer goods until the 1930s. Once plastic arrived, it became possible to create free and even abstract forms that were difficult to achieve in wood or metal. PeDe's plastic grinders are valued as opening a new horizon in this kind of modern design. Two molded plastic pieces were joined into one body, and a side grip was added to steady the force transmitted while the crank was turned. Early plastics were strong against heat but weak against impact, so they often cracked or broke when hard coffee beans were ground.

The first plastic coffee grinder made by PeDe has a base measuring 8 by 15 cm and a height of 22 cm. Its waist narrows so it can be gripped by hand, and a side handle was added for stabilization, creating a design unlike anything that had come before. Later, Leinbrok in East Germany and KYM in Iserlohn produced similar models. Plastic, which is now often criticized and targeted for elimination, once appeared as a revolutionary material welcomed with applause across the world.

11. A Coffee Grinder That Recorded 200 Years of One Family
Even in the days when coffee grinders were handmade, there were still "trends," visible in technical tendencies and preferred design styles. Early nineteenth-century grinders generally had wide openings; later, makers added lids or made them narrower and deeper. Grind-adjustment mechanisms were fitted inside the body and used screws to lift the burr slightly. Designers also enlarged the body and added small wings to the base plate so the grinder could be fixed in place more securely, reducing wobble. The crank was also given a curved form to lessen the force transmitted to the arm.

This grinder, made in that way, was used by one family for 200 years, passed down from generation to generation. The hardwood body shows many traces of woodworm, so it was maintained with repeated oiling, and the hopper was patched again and again as well. Used across generations, the grinder came to hold the story of a single family. After the elderly owner died without descendants, it was acquired by a collector.

12. A "Gift of Love" That Captured Its Era
In nineteenth-century Europe, the social group that drank coffee was still limited. At the time, a coffee grinder was treated as more than a simple utensil.

This grinder was used as part of someone's wedding dowry. The body was painted black with pigment, and brass plates were attached on all four sides to bring out a golden sheen. The ring supporting the axle connected to the crank was also made of thick brass, and the crank itself was carefully carved into a graceful form. A bird symbolizing the groom was engraved on the brass plate at the side, while a flower symbolizing the bride was engraved on the front. The couple's initials, J and K, were painstakingly inscribed on the front, and the rear brass plate records the year of their marriage as 1819. The love story of the couple who married 207 years ago seems to have been carried down to the present in the rich aroma of coffee.

Types of Coffee Grinders
1. Knee-Held Coffee Grinders
This term refers to relatively small coffee grinders designed to be held between the knees while seated. Many historical manual grinders fall into this category. In this type, the crank is always positioned on the top of the grinder, a layout that emerged when coffee grinders entered mass production in the mid-nineteenth century. The grinders produced in the early twentieth century are distinct from earlier handcrafted ones. Most used beech (genus Fagus) for the body. The hopper also became relatively flat for easier mass production, and the body took on a simplified cuboid form. Another characteristic is the use of standardized grounds drawers. As levers for adjusting grind size became common, the overall size of the product decreased. Because these models were difficult to hold steady by hand, they were typically used braced between the knees or under the arm, hence the name knee-held grinder. The German firm Peter Dienes (PeDe) is often regarded as setting the standard for this type.

2. Tabletop Coffee Grinders
This category includes relatively large coffee grinders. Grinding beans requires considerable force, and securing a grinder against the shaking caused by that force was an important technical challenge for makers at the time. Making the grinder body sturdier reduced movement, but it also made the grinder itself heavier and harder to hold in place by hand. Grinders designed to be fastened to a dining table or desk with screws or clamps are classified as tabletop grinders. Most were fitted with metal hardware so they could be screwed to the edge of a table. When the body was made of wood, the base plate was widened so it could be fixed to the table at two points, or a groove was cut into the center of the base so it could be secured. Side-driven coffee grinders introduced by PeDe and the heavy cast grinders made of molded metal can also be included in this category. Side-driven grinders began appearing after 1880.

3. Wall-Mounted Coffee Grinders
By the early twentieth century, coffee was finally on the path to becoming a mass-market drink. Roasters began selling beans in small packages, and households needed a convenient way to store them. Wall-mounted coffee grinders were "modern objects" in the sense that they made it possible to store and grind beans quickly in one place. For European housewives, a new coffee-drinking public, manufacturers decorated the bean-storage section with pretty ceramics and made the grounds drawer of glass. Wall-mounted ceramic grinders developed in the 1880s were produced by several makers in the early 1900s and became popular across Europe. These grinders were built by fixing a ceramic bean hopper and burr assembly onto a wooden board about 15 cm wide, 38 cm high, and 1 cm thick. When the crank on the front was turned clockwise, beans fell by gravity from the hopper into the burrs and, once ground, dropped into a glass drawer below. They were very popular because they reduced the space needed for storing and grinding beans, but their use declined once airtight containers were developed and because using them while attached to the wall was less convenient.

4. Hand Mills, Travel Grinders
Many coffee grinders from the early nineteenth century were cylindrical or small enough to fit in one hand, in the form now called a hand mill. They are still called hand mills because the user grips the body in one hand while operating them. These grinders were designed so the body could be held and braced by hand while force was applied during grinding. Because hand mills were meant for grinding small quantities of beans, they often incorporated the period's practical ergonomics so the grip area would fit comfortably in one hand. Portable brass examples from the Islamic world came to be known collectively as "Turkish mills," and they remain popular today.

In the twentieth century, hand mills began to be made in earnest from a wider range of new materials. Brass had previously been used to prevent corrosion, but with the appearance of chrome plating and the invention of plastic, it became much easier to create different sizes and shapes. As a result, portable hand mills that could be taken anywhere became widely available in many forms.

5. Wedding Gift Grinders
Among coffee grinders were special pieces known as "for the bride" or "wedding gift" grinders. As drinking coffee came to be treated as a refined cultural practice, grinders were included in wedding gifts sent by a groom to a bride and were also used as presents for couples getting married. Because a coffee grinder was both an everyday household object and a craft item that reflected the era's elite culture while producing the rich aroma of coffee, it was treated in its time as a "gift of love." The 1850s knee-held grinder in this exhibition is a wedding gift grinder decorated with gleaming brass, engraved with a bird and flower symbolizing the bride and groom, and inscribed with their initials.

6. Octagonal Cast-Iron Coffee Grinder AK&Son/1860/U.K.
This cast grinder, produced in Britain, the country that led the Industrial Revolution, takes the form of an eight-sided hand mill. For its time, it featured a strikingly bold design. Each face is decorated with flowers, and the top has an open hopper. Among collectors, it is said to have been modeled on the fountain at York Minster, though this is not certain. The base has a diameter of 12 cm, the height is 15 cm, and its cast construction makes it impressively heavy.

The manufacturer, Britain's AK & Sons Ltd., was founded in 1791 by Archibald Kenrick, who worked in the iron industry. The octagonal coffee grinder was designed in 1860 by Dr. Dresser (1834-1904). He is considered a pioneering figure of Art Nouveau and one of Britain's representative industrial designers. He was involved in the design of several AK & Sons products and helped express the image of an iron-making company through a domestic craft object called a coffee grinder. AK & Sons produced a range of cast products before closing its foundry in 1965. Remarkably, the company still exists today.

Individual Grinders
1. "Modern" Nordic Design
Tix/1900/Sweden. Tinplate, thin sheet iron coated with tin, was a breakthrough nineteenth-century method for preventing corrosion. The French inventor Nicolas Appert first introduced food packaging in tin cans in 1812.

Eskilstuna, about 90 minutes from Stockholm, was a city renowned for metalworking, and around 1900 several coffee-grinder manufacturers operated there, including C. Ramstedt & Co., Carl Zetterlund (1896-1915?), and Eskilstuna Stalindustri. Using tinplate and wood, they produced grinders of the same design and sold them under the same name. The tinplate grinder known as TIX resembles a mailbox and has a side crank. With a base area of 100 cm by 10 cm and a height of 16.5 cm, Tix used light tinplate to create a curved body, while its burr mechanism took the unusual form of several interlocking rollers. Distinct from earlier coffee grinders in both material and design, Tix was very popular not only in Sweden but across Northern Europe.

2. Pyramid Coffee Mill Ignaz Hauts & Sohn/1880/Austria
Austria's Steiermakt region was rich in mines from early times, and the iron ore produced there was sent throughout Europe and at times used in the production of coffee grinders.

The foundry Ignaz Hauts & Sohn, active in Vienna, made a cast grinder in 1851 whose pyramid-like design became very popular. Its success was so great that rival companies released coffee grinders with similar designs. In the late nineteenth century, the same company produced the design again with a wooden body instead of a cast one. The base measures 13 by 13 cm and the height is 21 cm, so it is not large, but because both the body and the burr mechanism were cast, it weighed about 900 g and felt quite heavy.

3. "Beechwood" Coffee Mill Zassenhaus331/1930/Germany
Beech is one of the most common deciduous broadleaf trees in the forests of Central Europe. It can grow to 50 m in height, 2 m in girth, and live for about 500 years. With a density of around 720 kg/m^3, it is a fairly heavy wood and has long been valued even as firewood and charcoal.

Model 331, produced in the 1930s by the German grinder maker Zassenhaus, has a bean hopper with a lid at the top and a chrome-plated finish. Its cubic body, 11 cm by 11 cm, is made of European beech. Oak and ash are heavy, harder to work with, and more sensitive to humidity, making them more difficult to maintain, whereas beech is strong, easy to work, and convenient to care for. Zassenhaus once sold more than 200,000 coffee grinders in a single year, and beech played an important role in that mass production. Zassenhaus's beech coffee grinder model 311 was remade and produced again in the 1950s and 1980s. Coffee grinders sold by Zassenhaus still use beech today.

4. Aluminum-Handle Coffee Grinder Gesto/1940/Germany
Manual coffee grinders became a declining business once electric grinders spread in the 1950s. The last new material to be applied to manual coffee grinders was aluminum. Aluminum entered mass production in 1887, but aluminum alloys did not become widespread until 1910 and were used in European coffee grinders in the 1940s.

The metal-products company founded by Joseph Stockert in Schwelm, Germany, changed its name to Gesto in 1922 and began producing coffee grinders. The firm took a strong interest in technological development and held many patents. The Mocka coffee grinder produced by Gesto in the 1930s kept the familiar rectangular design and used beech for the body, though later models were also made from ash and maple. The base measures 9 by 15 cm and the height is 21 cm. The hopper contains a Bakelite funnel, and the crank handle and hopper slide plate are made of aluminum. Gesto, a maker unafraid of new materials and design, stopped producing manual coffee grinders in 1956, the year electric coffee grinders appeared on the market.

5. Knee-Held Coffee Grinder
This term refers to coffee grinders designed to be held between the knees while seated. Many historical manual coffee grinders belong to this category. In this type, the crank is always positioned on the top of the grinder.

Knee-held grinders appeared in the mid-nineteenth century as grinder production entered a mass-production system. Coffee grinders made in the early twentieth century can be distinguished from earlier handcrafted examples, and most used beech for the body. The hopper also became relatively flat for easier mass production, and the body took on a simplified cuboid form. Another notable feature is the use of standardized grounds drawers. As levers for adjusting grind size became common, the overall size of the product decreased. Because these models were difficult to steady by hand, they were used in practice braced between the knees or under the arm. The German firm Peter Dienes (PeDe) is often regarded as having set the standard for the knee-held grinder.

6. Military Grinder, Field Grinder
Coffee grinders supplied to soldiers heading to the battlefield are meaningful not only as tools for making coffee but also as objects that record one facet of a historical era. The First and Second World Wars took place after coffee had already become a popular drink in Europe, and a variety of tools were developed to provide coffee to soldiers at the front. Coffee grinders had to work in field conditions, so they were made from sturdy materials.

The coffee grinder used by the Italian military in the field during the Second World War consists of a grinder and a case. The case measures 21 cm by 23 cm at the base and 135 cm in height. Inside the case is a cylindrical grinder with a diameter of 12 cm. Thick steel plate was used for military durability, bringing the total weight to 2.8 kg. Although the manufacturer of this military grinder is unclear, it is said to have been used later by the German military as well.

At the exhibition hall, you can also find coffee grinders used by the Swiss and German armies.

7. Elma - Spain
ELMA SA was founded in 1924 in Arrasate, Spain, by workers from the Mondragon Corporation, the largest employer in Spain's Basque region. Producing meat grinders, ice-cream machines, and coffee grinders, Elma began with 50 employees and grew into a company employing as many as 1,000 people in the 1930s. Founded chiefly by workers under the oppression and repression of the Franco dictatorship, Elma was sold in the 1940s. The ELMA SA building that still remains today has become a landmark in the Basque region.

ELMA SA produced coffee grinders only for a very short period, but the cast grinders made during that time are highly prized among collectors for their elegant design. Although intended for household use, they were cast in metal, fitted with a side flywheel, and finished in a reddish body color, becoming models that later inspired countless imitations.

8. Peter Dienes Germany/Netherlands
In 1869, Peter Dienes (born 1823) founded a company in Remscheid and later began producing coffee grinders under the brand name PEDE, whose logo featured a wooden cross between a deer's antlers. After Peter Dienes died in 1899, his two sons began full-scale coffee-grinder production. They sold German-made coffee grinders across Europe, and records suggest that PeDe's annual output in 1901 reached approximately 25,000 to 45,000 units.

In 1920, the company acquired a tobacco factory in Breukelen in the Netherlands and used it to manufacture products. In 1935 it obtained the patent for the world's first plastic coffee grinder, made with Bakelite. PeDe held a total of 14 patents related to coffee grinders. These included a patent for conical burrs and another for a cuboid tabletop grinder (model number 1710), once described as having the most complex grinding mechanism of its kind. For a company so deeply committed to grinding technology, it was the end of an era when PeDe removed all coffee-grinder production in 1970.
